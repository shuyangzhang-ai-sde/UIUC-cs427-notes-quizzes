Where to draw the diamond
Place the diamond on the whole side of the association. If there is an arrow, it points toward the part.

Multiplicity — how to read the numbers
Multiplicity 0..1 1..* 0..* 1..4
Multiplicity is written on the far end of the association from the element you are reasoning about: a number on end A answers “for one B, how many A’s?”
Rule of thumb
Whichever end shows the multiplicity, that multiplicity constrains that end.
Equivalently: a label on A’s side counts how many A instances relate to one B.
NotationMeaningExample
1Exactly one (often omitted as the default)Each city belongs to exactly one state
0..1Zero or one (optional)An employee has zero or one direct manager
0..*Zero or moreAn author may have no books
1..*One or moreAn order line item appears at least once
1..4Between one and four inclusiveEach article lists one to four authors
Reading the Week 5 quiz diagram
Article Author Book Publication
Read each end of an association on its own — the two multiplicities are independent constraints.
AssociationWhere the label sitsWhat it means
Article–AuthorOn the Author end: 1..4Each article has one to four authors
Article–AuthorOn the Article end: 0..*An author may write zero or more articles
Book–AuthorOn the Author end: 0..*Each book may list zero or more authors
Book–AuthorOn the Book end: 0..*An author may write zero or more books
What follows
Author end 0..* ⇒ “An author may have written nothing” is true.
Book end 0..* ⇒ “Not every book has an author” can be true.
So “Every publication has an author” is false in general.
Class diagrams — structure and role
Class diagram Attribute Operation Static structure
A class diagram is UML’s view of static structure: classes, attributes, operations, and the relationships between them.
Class
A classifier for things in the problem domain; drawn as a rectangle
Attribute
Structural data of the type — e.g. name, age
Operation
Behavior or services — e.g. order(), calculate()
What class diagrams do not show
They capture static structure only. They do not show:
  • Algorithmic or control-flow detail
  • Timing or real-time ordering
  • Step-by-step object interaction
Telling class, attribute, and operation apart
Class Attribute Operation
When you parse requirements into a model, use these cues:
ConstructWhat it isHow to spot it
ClassA stable concept you instantiateNoun; has identity and its own features
AttributeData owned by one classNoun scoped to a class; describes state
OperationBehavior the class performsVerb or verb phrase; “does something”
Pizza shop sketch
Requirement: customers order pizza or drinks; pizza has size, sauce, and crust/base choices.
  • Classes: Pizza, Customer, Drink, Sauce (for example)
  • Attributes: size, base type
  • Operation: order() — the act of ordering, not a stored entity
Order is the least likely to be modeled as its own class here; it fits an operation better.
Sequence diagrams

Week 5 — exam quick reference
Common pitfalls
  • Part–whole links use aggregation or composition, not generalization.
  • Generalization is inheritance (is-a): arrow from subclass to superclass, hollow triangle on the parent side.
  • Abstract classes show their names in italics and are not instantiated directly.
  • Multiplicity sits on the end it constrains (the “opposite” end from how you read the sentence).
  • A class diagram does not show timing or algorithms; use a sequence diagram (or activity, etc.).
  • On a sequence diagram, time runs down the page; participants sit across the top.
  • Event order follows trigger time, not “importance” or duration.
  • In the pizza scenario, order behaves like an operation, not a class.
  • Composition (filled diamond): part lifetime is tied to the whole; queue holding an internal array is the stock example.
